How it works

1. Recursive image. The source scene contains a smaller copy of itself, embedded by the similarity S(z) = c + a·z with scale |a| = ¼. This creates a picture-within-a-picture that repeats infinitely inward.

2. Log-polar transform. Mapping to ζ = ln r + i·θ converts the multiplicative self-similarity into additive periodicity: the image tiles with period ln(1/|a|) along the horizontal axis (radius) and 2π along the vertical axis (angle). Circles around the center become vertical lines; radial rays become horizontal lines.

3. Conformal alignment. Multiplying by a complex number α performs a rotation and isotropic scaling of the log-polar plane. The key insight: choosing α so that the vector from one copy to the next (one radial period + one full turn) maps to a pure vertical shift of 2πi makes the recursion seamlessly periodic. The sliders let you explore different α values; the presets compute the exact alignment for clockwise and counter-clockwise spirals.

4. Exponential map. Applying exp to the aligned ζ′ coordinates wraps the periodic strip back into polar form, producing the Droste spiral. Each pixel's screen position determines its ζ′ coordinates; the shader tiles, divides by α, and traces back through the recursion to sample the source texture. The result is a seamless spiral zoom that Escher approximated by hand in his 1956 lithograph Print Gallery.

Interact: Hover any panel to see corresponding structures across all four views — circles ↔ vertical lines, radial rays ↔ horizontal lines, spirals ↔ tilted lines. Drag to pan panel 4; scroll to zoom. Use the sliders or presets to change α in real time.

Based on the analysis of Escher's Print Gallery by H. Lenstra, B. de Smit, and collaborators at Leiden University. Built with WebGL 2.
